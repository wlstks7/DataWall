New Jersey

New Jersey's Immigration

Legal and Illegal Residence

2005 - 2012 | Source

Between 2005 and 2012, New Jersey received a little more than 3.5% of all unauthorized immigration and about 4.5% of legal permanent residents into the United States. New Jersey’s unauthorized immigration grew from 2005 to 2007 and fell until 2009. From 2009 to 2010, both pre and post-census data show an increase in unauthorized immigration, but post-census data reveals a much sharper increase. In 2011, immigration fell and then rose again in 2012. The odd-looking increase in 2010 is a comparison of the January 2010 unauthorized immigration report by the Department of Homeland Security and their amended estimations published in 2012 that estimated the unauthorized population using census data. Hovering the mouse on the left side of 2010 displays pre-census estimation and, on the right side, the post-census estimate. Post-census data reveals a 70,000 person underestimation of unauthorized immigrants in 2010, and the reporting methods seem to have adjusted for this in the years after.

Legal permanent residents increased from 2006 to 2007, decreased until 2009, and increased again through 2012. Legal permanent residents and unauthorized immigrants shared their lowest years in 2009. Unauthorized immigration peaked in 2007, and legal residents peaked in 2012.

Immigration From Mexico

How we found this.

Data of unauthorized immigration and legal permanent residents from Mexico both follow the overall immigration trends. Census data for unauthorized immigration from Mexico shows an interesting 23,784 person drop post-census though the overall immigration data increased.

Unauthorized Immigration Popularity
From 2005 to 2007, percentage and nominal immigration are both increasing. Regardless of the U.S. total immigration, the state’s popularity increased. From 2008 to 2009, both the percentage and nominal are both decreasing as well as its popularity. Pre-census data shows an increase in popularity, but post-census data showed a percentage decrease. The nominal increased, but the state still experienced a lower total amount of immigration than normal. In 2011, New Jersey continued to decrease its popularity and then increased in 2012.

Legal Permanent Resident Popularity
From 2006 to 2007, though nominal increased, the state experienced a lower amount of immigration than uniform. In 2008, nominal decreased, but the overall percentage remained from 2007. New Jersey experienced the expected nominal totals of immigration. In 2009, both percentage and nominal decreased. The small increase in 2010 was expected as the percentage remained the same as 2009. In 2011, both the percentage and nominal population increased, and the continued nominal increase into 2012 was expected as the percentage remained.
